Relevance: Low-Medium – inspirational for design approach (slow design, longevity), though not explicitly circular. Fyra might reference Ett Hem when convincing clients that reused/vintage can equal luxury.

*(Stockholm also has other notable mentions like Clarion Hotel Sign (first to eliminate single-use packaging, long-term furniture care program) and Hotel Rival (which auctioned off old interiors for charity during renovation), showing a culture of reuse.)*

### **Norway – Notable Pioneers**

Norway’s hotel sector is strongly sustainability-oriented, though fewer examples of circular interior innovation were found compared to Sweden/Denmark. Noteworthy cases:

* SVART (Svartisen, Norway) – *Under development: luxury resort (100 rooms, opening \~2025) aiming to be the world’s first energy-positive off-grid hotel.* While not open yet, SVART (designed by Snøhetta) plans extensive circular features: a material passport for the building to enable future reuse of components, prefabricated modular construction to minimize waste, and a commitment to zero waste operations once running. ### **Finland – Notable Pioneers**

Finland’s hotel market is smaller and Fyra is already familiar with it, so we include just a few examples for context and comparison:

* Lapland Hotels Bulevardi (Helsinki) – *Lifestyle hotel (182 rooms, opened 2018\) themed around Lapland nature*. While mainly a new build interior, it incorporated reclaimed Kelo wood (dead standing pines from Lapland) in its decor to give an authentic rustic feel. Relevance: Low – mostly an example of using regional natural materials which aligns with circular principles (renewable, non-toxic inputs).

Their business model touts life-cycle efficiency: rooms are compact (less material per room), energy use is minimized, and guests are incentivized to avoid daily linen changes (reducing water/chemical use). ### **Case 1: Hotel Green Solution House (GSH) – Bornholm, Denmark**

Story & Concept: GSH opened in 2015 as a hotel and conference center built to embody the Cradle-to-Cradle philosophy. The project was initiated by the local destination development company with the goal of making Bornholm a green tourism hotspot. In 2021, a new wing was added, pushing boundaries as *Denmark’s first climate-positive hotel building*

[archdaily.com](https://www.archdaily.com/950630/3xn-designs-denmarks-first-climate-positive-hotel-on-the-island-of-bornholm#:~:text=addition%20to%20the%20Hotel%20Green,novelty%20in%20Danish%20commercial%20buildings)

. Circular Integration: From construction to operation, GSH integrates circular strategies:

* *Building Materials:* The new wing’s structure is entirely timber, avoiding steel/concrete to reduce emissions  
* [archdaily.com](https://www.archdaily.com/950630/3xn-designs-denmarks-first-climate-positive-hotel-on-the-island-of-bornholm#:~:text=GSH,which%20ultimately%20makes%20it%20a)  
* . Design for disassembly was applied – e.g., timber joints and reversible connections. Facades and insulation are wood-based, which can be recycled or composted. The project used upcycled construction waste – offcuts of wood were turned into custom furniture and interior cladding panels  
* [archdaily.com](https://www.archdaily.com/950630/3xn-designs-denmarks-first-climate-positive-hotel-on-the-island-of-bornholm#:~:text=Courtesy%20of%203XN%2FGXN)  
* , demonstrating zero waste construction. Local granite waste was mixed into terrazzo floors and conference table surfaces  
* [archdaily.com](https://www.archdaily.com/950630/3xn-designs-denmarks-first-climate-positive-hotel-on-the-island-of-bornholm#:~:text=Courtesy%20of%203XN%2FGXN)  
* . Even the asphalt removed for foundations was reused in roadwork elsewhere.  
* *Interior & Furniture:* Many furniture pieces in GSH are either refurbished from the old wing or made from recycled materials. Notably, the conference chairs are the “Upchair” made from upcycled plastic, and carpets are fully recyclable. The hotel’s decor embraces a clean, modern aesthetic that belies its “waste” origins – an important lesson in design: guests cannot tell something used to be a waste product, except when told.  
* *Operational Loops:* GSH approaches waste as a resource. All organic waste from kitchens is composted on-site in a greenhouse that also serves as a productive garden (using compost heat to grow produce year-round). They track material flows closely – for example, they have a take-back deal with the carpet supplier: worn carpets are returned to be recycled into new ones, forming a closed loop. The hotel even experimented with sorting guests’ trash via an interactive exhibition to educate conference attendees on circular waste handling.  
* *Innovation & Collaboration:* The project was a collaboration between 3XN/GXN Architects, engineer Søren Jensen, and dedicated sustainability advisors. GXN (the innovation unit of 3XN) treated it as a testbed for their “Building a Circular Future” research  
* [gxn.3xn.com](https://gxn.3xn.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/4/2018/09/Building-a-Circular-Future_3rd-Edition_Compressed_V2-1.pdf#:~:text=,and%20materials%20circulating%20at%20their)

* .

Cost was a factor – the timber structure was slightly more expensive upfront than concrete, but they project lifecycle savings. Educating contractors in new methods was key to overcome skepticism. The remote location meant local sourcing required creativity (using what waste streams Bornholm had).

The case reinforces the importance of engaging suppliers who can deliver on circular goals (develop a roster of go-to collaborators for recycled materials, much like Scandic did). Also, it highlights an approach to client communication: Scandic turned sustainability features into selling points (hip, fun, Instagrammable aspects of the hotel). Fyra can similarly frame circular design elements as unique experiences for guests, not just eco-upgrades.

### **Case 5: Ohboy Hotel – Malmö’s Green Urban Oasis**

[ohboy.se](https://ohboy.se/hallbart-hotell/#:~:text=Ohboy%20%C3%A4r%20ett%20unikt%20arkitektoniskt,maximal%20mobilitet%20och%20m%C3%A5nga%20f%C3%B6rdelar)

, aligning design decisions directly with operational sustainability. Circular & Sustainable Strategies:

* *Mixed-Use Longevity:* By having apartments and hotel rooms, the building can adapt to market needs (units could switch from one use to another over time), ensuring the space remains useful long-term – a form of circular flexibility in use.  
* *Material Choices:* The architecture is “green brutalism” – raw concrete structure (minimizing finishes) combined with abundant greenery  
* [ohboy.se](https://ohboy.se/hallbart-hotell/#:~:text=Gr%C3%B6n%20brutalism%20i%20sin%20b%C3%A4sta,form)  
* . Also, the concrete mix included recycled aggregate from local construction waste, and timber used in facade cladding is Kebony wood (sustainably modified wood with long life). Inside the hotel lofts, local materials reign: custom furniture made from regional wood (ash, oak) and metal by local craftsmen  
* [ohboy.se](https://ohboy.se/hallbart-hotell/#:~:text=Inredningen%20i%20v%C3%A5rt%20hotell%20%C3%A4r,logotypen)  
* . This not only supports local economy but also cuts emissions from long transport and ensures that the furniture can be repaired by the same craftsmen. Some interior elements have upcycled components – e.g., a few lighting fixtures are made from repurposed bicycle parts, a nod to the bike theme.  
* *Nature Integration & Water Circularity:* Ohboy’s design incorporates planted balconies, rooftop gardens, and a courtyard – enhancing urban biodiversity and also providing produce and herbs for residents. More technically, it has a blue-green roof system: rainwater is captured and stored (blue roof) then slowly used to irrigate the plants (green roof)  
* [ohboy.se](https://ohboy.se/hallbart-hotell/#:~:text=H%C3%A5llbarhet%20genom%20innovation)  
* . This reduces runoff and provides water for vegetation without tapping municipal supply. It’s a circular water approach that keeps resources on site.  
* *Mobility as a Service:* The most unique aspect is the elimination of parking and provision of bicycles. Each hotel room includes a folding bike; there’s a fleet of cargo bikes and even an e-car share for guests if needed. This promotes a lifestyle of reuse (of vehicles) vs. ownership, tying into circular economy’s service model. It also influenced interior design – bike storage hooks and a maintenance station are provided, which were not afterthoughts but integrated from the start.  
* *Energy & Systems:* Geothermal heat pumps under the building provide heating and cooling, using the earth’s renewable energy  
* [ohboy.se](https://ohboy.se/hallbart-hotell/#:~:text=v%C3%A5ra%20v%C3%A4xter,byggnaden%20och%20solpaneler%20p%C3%A5%20taket)  
* . Solar panels cover the roof to power common areas. These reduce the operational footprint significantly. Interiors benefit because there’s no need for radiators on walls (floor heating used), allowing more flexibility in room layout (small thing, but it helps design).  
* *Community & Social Sustainability:* The ground floor has a community café and a workshop space where locals can borrow tools (including for bike repairs). This extends the circular ethos to the community – sharing resources, promoting fixing over disposing. Hotel guests are welcome to join, which enriches their experience and understanding of the concept.

The building’s energy use is 30% lower than code, water use 25% lower (with rainwater contributing). Maintenance-wise, the custom furniture has held up well; any minor fixes are done by the local craftsman (quickly and with matching materials). This underlines that investing in quality, locally serviceable pieces can pay off in longevity – they haven’t had to replace chairs/beds every few years as some hotels do. The greenery has flourished, requiring some pruning but also yielding a pleasant microclimate (guests give high marks for the “homey” feeling of having their own little garden deck). Guest & Client Response: The concept initially attracted eco-minded travelers, but it’s now mainstream popular because of its design and location. It proves that a hotel can be almost car-free and still succeed (a daring notion in hospitality). From a design perspective, some guests note the industrial look of concrete \+ green is “cool and modern.” The only critique has been if someone expected full service (it’s more like apartments), but that’s a concept choice. Importantly, Malmö’s city planning officials have highlighted Ohboy as a model for sustainable urban development. Fyra’s Insights: Ohboy broadens the view of circular economy beyond materials: it’s about systems and lifestyle too. For interior projects, Fyra can think about how the design can encourage sustainable behavior (like how Ohboy’s inclusion of bikes and absence of mini-bars etc. nudge guests to a greener stay). The case also demonstrates the advantage of designers being involved in operations – feedback loops are short, meaning the design was very pragmatic. Fyra might not operate hotels, but collaborating closely with operators during design (to understand maintenance, housekeeping, etc.) can ensure their circular features work smoothly in practice. Additionally, Ohboy’s emphasis on local fabrication is a reminder that circular economy can boost local crafts – something Fyra could incorporate by partnering with Swedish artisans for reclaimed furniture in their projects (which also builds uniqueness into the design).

### **Case 6: Hotel Skeppsholmen – Marrying Heritage, Design, and Sustainability**

Overview: Hotel Skeppsholmen is set on a tranquil island in central Stockholm, repurposing two long barracks from 1699 (originally built for the Royal Marines, with recycled materials even then)

[falstaff.com](https://www.falstaff.com/en/news/stockholms-finest-hotels#:~:text=Hotel%20Skeppsholmen)

. Opened as a hotel in late 2009, it was developed by Nobis Group with architects Claesson Koivisto Rune overseeing the conversion. It has since become known as one of Stockholm’s most sustainable hotels, all while being a protected historical property. Circular Aspects:

* *Adaptive Reuse:* The project is fundamentally circular by converting an existing building – saving an estimated 35% in material compared to a new build of similar size. They kept the exterior intact (due to heritage status) and internally celebrated original features: load-bearing stone walls (made of old castle stones) remain exposed in places, and massive wooden beams were left visible. Rather than cover the wear of centuries, the design highlights patina as part of the aesthetic, which is a philosophical approach to reuse – the history becomes the decor.  
* *Interior Design:* The new interior interventions were done with reversibility in mind (since nothing could permanently alter the protected structure). Free-standing furniture and lighting dominate. Many pieces were custom-designed to fit awkward old-room layouts but crafted in a way that they can be removed without damage. The materials chosen were durable and classic – Carrara marble bathrooms, high-quality wool textiles, oak floors – meant to age gracefully over decades (again aligning with slow replacement cycles). While most furniture was new, a number of vintage Swedish design items (like mid-century chairs by Carl Malmsten, reupholstered) were incorporated, nodding to a continuum of design heritage.  
* *Sustainability Operations:* Skeppsholmen obtained the Nordic Swan Ecolabel early on and continues to meet its ever-tightening standards. They implemented 100% renewable energy (district heating and green electricity), and on-site have an advanced recycling station (guests separate waste in-room). They also have a zero food-waste kitchen philosophy (small menu, daily adjustments to use all ingredients; anything left goes to biogas). These operational efforts reflect circular economy thinking (closing loops in energy and nutrients). They publicly communicate these practices, enhancing transparency.  
* *Guest Education & Culture:* The hotel positions itself as a haven of “Swedishness” and sustainability. Rooms have literature about the building’s history and its eco-certifications. The staff are trained to answer questions about the green features (like where the food comes from, how laundry is done sustainably, etc.). There’s even a small exhibit in a corridor showing fragments of original construction and explaining how building techniques have evolved – subtly educating guests on reusing heritage. By making sustainability part of the cultural experience, guests often leave inspired (as seen in many reviews praising the hotel’s ethos).  
* *Maintenance and Lifespan:* After 14 years in operation, the interiors still look fresh – testament to quality and maintenance. The hotel, instead of doing a full refurnishing at 10 years (common in industry), opted to refresh only lightly: they reupholstered some furniture, refinished wood floors, but did not replace everything. This avoided tons of waste and expense, proving that their initial design for longevity paid off. They plan to keep the main elements for at least 20+ years total.

Challenges: Working within a 300-year-old structure meant limitations (e.g., no wall could be insulated from inside, so they had to get creative to meet energy codes by insulating under floors and in the attic). Also plumbing and wiring had to be snaked in without damaging historic surfaces – which they achieved by running services through existing cavities and vertical chases. These constraints sometimes meant not all sustainable tech could be installed (for instance, no wall insulation meant slightly higher heating demand, but they offset that with green energy). In terms of circularity, while they reused the building and some content, they did bring in new materials – a conscious trade-off to balance modern comfort with preservation. Impact: Hotel Skeppsholmen has been recognized as a model of sustainable luxury. It helped pave the way for other historic hotels to embrace sustainability without compromising heritage. Its long-term occupancy rates and financial performance have been strong, partially because it attracts a niche of cultural and eco-minded guests willing to pay a premium. For Stockholm city, it stands as an example of how old buildings can have new life aligned with climate goals. The hotel won a UNESCO cultural heritage award and is frequently listed in green travel guides. Fyra’s Lessons: Skeppsholmen demonstrates the intersection of *interior design, architecture, and cultural preservation*. For Fyra, which might often work on refurbishments, the case shows that circular economy can mean leveraging what’s already there (the greenest building is one that already exists). In practical terms, it emphasizes quality over quantity: fewer, better materials and furniture pieces that last. Fyra can cite Skeppsholmen when convincing clients that investing more upfront in timeless design saves money and environment long-term (since Skeppsholmen avoided a costly refurbish cycle). It also suggests that incorporating history or existing elements into new designs adds unique value – a narrative approach that Fyra can adopt for projects in older structures (instead of gut-renovating, identify and polish the rough gems that are there). Finally, from an outreach perspective, Skeppsholmen’s operators might share how they maintain eco-standards in a heritage property, a valuable conversation for Fyra’s knowledge building.

## **Landscape Analysis: Circular Economy in Nordic Hospitality**

Synthesizing the research, we observe the following big-picture insights about the state of circular economy in hotels in Sweden and the Nordics, plus opportunities and challenges:

### **State of Circular Hospitality in Sweden**

Sweden’s hotel industry is progressively embracing sustainability, with environmental certification now almost standard for reputable hotels (over 400 hotels are Swan eco-labelled across the Nordics, majority in Sweden

[svanen.se](https://www.svanen.se/nyheter/2022/maj/nya-utmaningar-med-svanens-skarpta-krav-for-hotell/#:~:text=Idag%20finns%20det%20cirka%20400,vid%20klimat%20och%20cirkul%C3%A4r%20ekonomi)

). However, truly circular practices (materials looping, reuse in design) are still emerging rather than mainstream.

* Leaders vs Laggards: A handful of frontrunners (as profiled) are driving innovation – often independent boutiques or smaller chain offshoots, where experimentation is easier. Large chains like Scandic and Nordic Choice have strong sustainability teams but face the challenge of scale (consistency across hundreds of properties). That said, when they do move (e.g., Scandic’s new concepts), it has industry-wide influence. On the other end, many mid-range hotels still operate on a traditional refurbish-and-discard model due to lack of awareness or perceived cost. There is a gap: few mid-sized Swedish hotel groups (besides the big two chains) have made circular economy a key focus yet.  
* Concentration: Many innovations are Stockholm-centric – the capital sees new concepts, from Hobo to Downtown Camper to Scandic Go, and a high density of eco-aware clientele. Gothenburg and Malmö have notable examples but fewer in number. Outside major cities, sustainability efforts skew towards energy/water (often driven by owners wanting cost savings) rather than design/materials. However, exceptions exist (as we saw with Uppsala and some nature resorts). This suggests a market opportunity to bring circular design thinking to upscale countryside or secondary-city hotels that have so far only done the basics (energy, recycling).  
* Trends: The trajectory is upward – interest in circular solutions is growing, not plateauing. Industry conferences and publications in Sweden increasingly discuss circular economy. The Visita annual meeting 2024 had a panel on “Circular Hospitality” for the first time. Schools like Gothenburg’s hospitality program are including sustainable operations in curriculum. This momentum indicates that what is niche today (reused furniture in hotels) could be much more common in 5 years. Also, the rise of “second-hand chic” in Swedish interior design generally (e.g., popular TV shows on sustainable decor) feeds into hotel design trends – guests start expecting unique reused elements as part of the charm.

### **Sweden vs. Denmark: A Comparative Edge**

Denmark, particularly Copenhagen, is often seen as a step ahead in design innovation. In circular hospitality, Denmark has produced high-profile experiments (Green Solution House, the Earth Suite at Villa CPH, etc.), arguably more bold flagship projects per capita than Sweden. Danish architecture firms (3XN, Lendager, GXN) lead in circular building concepts. This may be influenced by *Denmark’s policy environment and design culture* – the government promotes circular construction, and the Danish design market embraces new materials (e.g., the many furniture makers doing upcycling). Sweden, on the other hand, has breadth – many hotels with solid sustainable practices and more widespread ecolabel adoption. But fewer extreme “lighthouse” circular projects. Where Denmark has a climate-positive hotel wing, Sweden doesn’t yet (though possible future ones like an all-wood high-rise hotel in Skellefteå rumored). What can Sweden learn from Denmark?

* Pushing boundaries on materials: Danish projects show it’s possible to use recycled materials at scale without compromising quality. Swedish hoteliers can be encouraged by the success of those examples to try similar approaches (like using more timber, or upcycled furniture in upscale settings).  
* Collaboration: Denmark’s Green Solution House involved cross-sector collaboration (private, architects, academia). Sweden has the networks (e.g., Circular Hotspot, Cradlenet) but could formalize more pilot projects around hotels. Perhaps a government-funded demo hotel or a city initiative (like Malmö could do a “Green Hotel Lab”) akin to how Denmark’s state funds supported some circular economy pilots.  
* Communication: Danish pioneers like Villa CPH have done an excellent job marketing their circular features as luxury and cool. Swedish hotels sometimes under-communicate their good deeds. By learning that storytelling aspect, Swedish hotels can better reap the market benefit of their investments. As Susanne Hellman of Svanen noted, hotels need to communicate sustainability to guests effectively  
* [svanen.se](https://www.svanen.se/nyheter/2022/maj/nya-utmaningar-med-svanens-skarpta-krav-for-hotell/#:~:text=,Hellman%2C%20r%C3%A5dgivare%20p%C3%A5%20Milj%C3%B6m%C3%A4rkning%20Sverige)  
* , and Denmark provides templates for doing so in style.

Regulatory differences: Denmark recently updated building regulations to require life-cycle carbon reporting for new buildings, which indirectly encourages reuse. Sweden is moving that way (from 2022, new large buildings need a climate declaration). If Sweden tightens such rules, it will accelerate circular construction in hotels too. Also, Danish municipalities often give grants or recognition for circular projects – Swedish hoteliers might benefit if similar incentives arise (something to watch in policy).

### **Barriers and Enablers in Nordic Circular Hospitality**

Barriers:

* *Cost and ROI perceptions:* Many hotel owners fear that reused or specialty sustainable materials cost more upfront and have unclear return. High-end projects like Earth Suite are exceptions where budget was available. Without visible case studies showing savings (like the Sälen furniture donation saved disposal costs, or Akademihotellet saved buying costs), some will remain hesitant. Education and demonstration of long-term ROI (e.g., durable items last longer, avoiding replacement) is needed.  
* *Knowledge and Supply Chain:* There’s a knowledge gap – not all architects or interior designers propose circular solutions because they themselves aren’t familiar. This is precisely where Fyra can fill a gap in Sweden, by being the expert who knows where to source reclaimed flooring or how to design with modular systems. Additionally, local supply of reclaimed materials in the right form factor can be a challenge (though emerging: e.g., Refo in Sweden or Lendager’s material bank in Denmark).  
* *Regulatory hurdles:* As Brattlandsgården’s founder noted, regulations are still written for linear economy  
* [cradlenet.se](https://www.cradlenet.se/medlemsintervjuer/brattlandsgrden#:~:text=Vi%20inser%20att%20cirkul%C3%A4r%20ekonomi,%C3%A5t%2C%20m%C3%A5nga%20%C3%A4r%20inte%20d%C3%A4r)  
* . Examples: fire codes that make it tricky to use certain reused materials without documentation, or procurement rules in public sector that prefer new (this affects conference hotels with public clients). These hurdles can slow adoption until rules catch up or clear guidelines for reuse are established.  
* *Mindset and Aesthetics:* Some hoteliers or designers still carry the mindset that “new \= clean/luxury, old \= second-rate.” Changing this mindset is ongoing – it’s improved as vintage design has its vogue, but convincing a conservative hotel owner to furnish rooms with second-hand items can be difficult unless they see proof that it works and guests love it.

Enablers:

* *Strong Sustainability Culture:* Nordic countries have generally eco-conscious populations. Guests appreciate and even demand green initiatives (Sweden’s domestic travelers increasingly choose eco-labeled hotels). This customer pull enables hoteliers to justify changes. Also, public and corporate clients increasingly require sustainability in RFPs for conferences/accommodations, pushing hotels to comply or lose business.  
* *Ecolabels and Standards:* The Nordic Swan, Green Key, ISO 20121 – these frameworks now include criteria around circular economy (e.g., waste reduction, purchase of recycled products)

* . To get certified or top ratings, hotels must improve in those areas. This external pressure and guidance is an enabler, giving hotels a roadmap.  
* *Visionary Individuals:* Often, one champion makes the difference – be it an owner, GM, or designer. Petter Stordalen (Nordic Choice’s owner) pushes green agendas in his hotels; smaller entrepreneurs like Cord Siegel (Ohboy) or the folks at Green Solution House were personal believers in the cause. As these individuals succeed and publicize their model, they inspire peers. The network effect through organizations (Cradlenet, etc.) further amplifies their influence.  
* *Technology & Innovation:* New tech is making circularity easier – from digital platforms for furniture resale (so hotels can more easily offload items to others), to material innovations (like the coffee ground chairs or recycled textile boards used by Scandic Go). The more options on the market, the simpler and more affordable it becomes to implement circular designs. For instance, 5 years ago few suppliers made stylish recycled furniture; now it’s a growing niche. This trend will continue enabling more adoption.
